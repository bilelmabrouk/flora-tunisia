Glossary

LoamA balanced soil of sand, silt and clay that drains well yet holds moisture and nutrients — ideal for most plants.
Well-drainedSoil or a pot that lets excess water flow away quickly so roots do not sit in water and rot.
DeadheadRemoving spent flowers to encourage more blooms and keep the plant tidy.
NodeThe point on a stem where leaves, buds or roots grow — where you usually cut for cuttings.
Hardening offGradually getting an indoor-raised plant used to outdoor sun and wind over a week or two before planting out.
MulchA layer of material (compost, bark, gravel) over the soil to keep moisture in, cool roots and cut weeds — vital in Tunisia's heat.
PerennialA plant that lives for several years, coming back each season.
AnnualA plant that completes its life in one season, then dies.
Drought-tolerantAble to survive long dry spells once established — a key trait for much of Tunisia.
HardinessHow much cold a plant can survive, usually given as a minimum temperature.
PropagationMaking new plants — from seed, cuttings, division or layering.
DecoctionA herbal preparation made by simmering roots, bark or seeds in water — see the preparation-methods page.
InfusionPouring hot water over leaves or flowers and letting them steep, like a tea.
pHHow acid or alkaline the soil is. Many Tunisian soils are alkaline (high pH), which can lock up iron for some plants.
ChlorosisYellowing leaves with green veins, often from iron shortage in alkaline soil — common with citrus here.
