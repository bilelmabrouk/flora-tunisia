Pindo / jelly palm

Butia odorata  ·  Family: Arecaceae
Trees · Palm / statement
EN Pindo / jelly palm  ·  FR Palmier abricot  ·  AR بوتيا  ·  Butia odorata
Origin: Southern Brazil and Uruguay (grasslands and open woodlands)
Light
Full sun
Water
Low
Care
Moderate
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short The pindo palm is a compact, cold-hardy feather-leaved palm that produces clusters of fragrant, edible orange fruits used to make jelly. Drought-tolerant and adaptable, it suits Tunisian gardens from the north to the Sahel.
Light
Full sun — needs maximum direct sun for best fruiting and blue-green leaf colour.
Water
Low to moderate: drought-tolerant once established; deep watering in dry summers improves fruit.
Soil
Well-drained sandy or loamy soil; tolerates slightly alkaline or slightly acidic; no waterlogging.
Temperature
Hardy to −10 °C; tolerates heat; one of the most cold-tolerant feather palms.
Humidity
Tolerates coastal humidity and dry inland conditions.
Feeding
Palm fertilizer twice a year — spring and late summer; essential in poor soils.
Pruning & grooming
Remove only completely dead fronds; do not cut the natural upward curve; petioles are spiny.
Propagation
From seed; slow — 3–6 months to germinate.
Mature size
3–6 m tall; crown 4–6 m spread.
Growth rate
Slow — 20–40 cm per year.
Lifespan
Long-lived; 80–100+ years.
Bloom / fruit season
Cream to yellow-red inflorescences in spring–early summer; orange-yellow fruits ripen in summer.
Hardiness (min temp)
Hardy to −10 °C — among the most cold-tolerant feather palms for Mediterranean gardens.
Toxicity & safety
Non-toxic; fruits edible and used for jelly-making.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) A slow-release palm fertilizer with potassium, magnesium, and chelated iron applied twice a year prevents the yellowing that can affect this palm in alkaline Tunisian soils, and supports good fruit production.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Frond yellowing starting from the older leavesPotassium or magnesium deficiency, particularly in sandy soils.Apply palm fertilizer; mulch to retain soil moisture and nutrients.
Fruits dropping before ripeningIrregular watering during fruit development.Maintain consistent moisture from fruit set to ripening.
Spear rot — central new frond turning brown and mushyBacterial or fungal infection of the growing point, often from waterlogging.Improve drainage immediately; apply a copper-based fungicide to the growing point.
Availability: Occasionally available in Tunisian nurseries; more common in specialised or well-stocked garden centres.
