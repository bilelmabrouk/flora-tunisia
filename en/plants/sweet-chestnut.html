Sweet chestnut

Castanea sativa  ·  Family: Fagaceae
Trees · Fruit / nut
EN Sweet chestnut  ·  FR Châtaignier  ·  AR كستناء  ·  Castanea sativa
Origin: Southern Europe and western Asia (from Iberia to the Caucasus)
Light
Full sun
Water
Low
Care
Moderate
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short A stately deciduous tree producing edible chestnuts, suited to the cool, wetter north and north-west of Tunisia. Needs chilling hours in winter and tolerates frost well.
Light
Full sun to light partial shade; best production in full sun with shelter from strong wind.
Water
Moderate; tolerates dry spells once established but fruits better with regular moisture in summer.
Soil
Deep, slightly acidic to neutral, well-drained soil; dislikes alkaline or waterlogged conditions.
Temperature
Hardy; needs 400–700 chilling hours below 7 °C for good nut production. Tolerates −15 °C.
Humidity
Adapts to the humid north; summer dryness is tolerated when roots are deep.
Feeding
Light annual feed with a balanced fertilizer in late winter before bud break.
Pruning & grooming
Minimal pruning needed; remove crossing or dead branches in late winter.
Propagation
From fresh seed (sow immediately after harvest); grafting for named varieties.
Mature size
Can reach 20–30 m in the wild; garden specimens kept to 8–15 m.
Growth rate
Moderate; slow in youth then faster once established.
Lifespan
Very long-lived — centuries if conditions suit.
Bloom / fruit season
Inconspicuous catkins in May–June; edible nuts ripen in autumn.
Hardiness (min temp)
Very frost-hardy (to around −15 °C); thrives in regions 1 and 2.
Toxicity & safety
Safe — nuts are a traditional food; raw young leaves and bark may mildly upset stomach if eaten.

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Brown, rotting nuts before maturityChestnut blight fungus (Cryphonectria parasitica) or chestnut weevil larvae inside.Remove and destroy affected nuts and cankers; choose resistant varieties.
Yellowing leaves with green veinsIron chlorosis from alkaline or calcareous soil.Acidify soil slightly with sulphur; apply chelated iron.
Cankers and die-back on branchesChestnut blight canker on bark.Cut out affected wood well below canker; disinfect tools between cuts.
Availability: Available from specialist nurseries in northern Tunisia; less common in the south.
