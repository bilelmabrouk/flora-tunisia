Banana

Musa × paradisiaca  ·  Family: Musaceae
Trees · Fruit / nut
EN Banana  ·  FR Bananier  ·  AR موز  ·  Musa × paradisiaca
Origin: South-East Asia (hybrid of Musa acuminata and M. balbisiana)
Light
Full sun
Water
High
Care
Demanding
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short A tropical giant that can fruit in Tunisia's warmest coastal gardens and southern oases. Bananas need heat, generous water and protection from wind — rewarding in the right microclimate.
Light
Full sun all day; any significant shade dramatically reduces growth and fruiting.
Water
High; water deeply and regularly — the banana is essentially a water-storing herb. Reduce in cooler months.
Soil
Rich, well-drained, moisture-retaining loam; incorporate compost generously; cannot tolerate waterlogging.
Temperature
Grows vigorously above 20 °C; growth slows below 15 °C; pseudostems die back below 5 °C; roots survive mild frost.
Humidity
Prefers high humidity; mulch heavily to retain moisture and buffer soil temperature.
Feeding
Heavy feeder; apply a high-potassium fertilizer monthly during the growing season.
Pruning & grooming
Remove dead leaves regularly; cut down the fruiting pseudostem after harvest; leave 1–2 suckers to replace it.
Propagation
By division of suckers from the base.
Mature size
2–5 m tall (pseudostem height); huge paddle-shaped leaves.
Growth rate
Very fast when warm and well-watered — can go from sucker to harvest in 9–18 months.
Lifespan
Individual pseudostems die after fruiting; the clump persists indefinitely.
Bloom / fruit season
Large pendulous inflorescence matures into a hand of bananas; fruit in 9–12 months after flowering.
Hardiness (min temp)
Very frost-tender; foliage killed by any frost; suited to frost-free regions 1, 3, 5, 6.
Toxicity & safety
Fully safe; fruit, flower and all parts edible.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) Bananas are heavy feeders; a potassium-rich fertilizer applied monthly during the growing season is essential for good fruit development — potassium deficiency causes poor, undersized bunches.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Pale streaks on young leaves (Panama wilt)Fusarium oxysporum cubense soil fungus — Panama disease.Use resistant varieties (Cavendish types); avoid replanting in infected soil for several years.
Torn, shredded leavesStrong wind damage — very common in exposed Tunisian coastal sites.Plant in a sheltered spot or install a windbreak; torn leaves are cosmetic only and do not harm the plant.
Yellow-green mottle on leaves (virus)Banana mosaic virus spread by aphids.Remove and destroy infected plants; control aphids; use clean, virus-free planting material.
Availability: Available in nurseries mainly in coastal Tunisia; suckers traded informally between gardeners.
