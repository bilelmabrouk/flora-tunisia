Quince

Cydonia oblonga  ·  Family: Rosaceae
Trees · Fruit / nut
EN Quince  ·  FR Cognassier  ·  AR سفرجل  ·  Cydonia oblonga
Origin: South-western Asia and the Caucasus region
Light
Full sun
Water
Low
Care
Moderate
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short A hardy deciduous fruit tree with beautiful spring blossom, widely grown in Tunisia for its aromatic quince fruit used in preserves and traditional cooking. Tolerates mild frost and moderate drought.
Light
Full sun for best fruiting; tolerates very light partial shade but yields are reduced.
Water
Moderate; water regularly during establishment and summer fruiting; drought-tolerant once mature.
Soil
Adaptable to most soils; prefers deep, fertile, well-drained loam; tolerates clay better than most fruit trees.
Temperature
Hardy to −15 °C; needs 300–500 chilling hours below 7 °C for flowering and fruiting.
Humidity
Average; copes with dry Tunisian summers once established.
Feeding
Balanced fertilizer in early spring just before buds open; avoid excess nitrogen.
Pruning & grooming
Open-vase pruning in late winter; remove crossing and inward branches annually.
Propagation
By hardwood cuttings in winter or grafting onto quince rootstock.
Mature size
3–6 m tall and wide; can be trained smaller.
Growth rate
Moderate; starts fruiting from year 3–5.
Lifespan
Long-lived — 50–100 years with good care.
Bloom / fruit season
Attractive white-pink flowers in March–April; golden-yellow fruit in October–November.
Hardiness (min temp)
Frost-hardy to about −15 °C; well-suited to highland and northern Tunisia.
Toxicity & safety
Fruit flesh is safe; the seeds contain amygdalin — avoid eating them in large quantities.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) Apply a balanced fruit-tree fertilizer in early spring; avoid excess nitrogen which encourages leaves over fruit.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Orange-rust pustules on leavesQuince rust fungus (Gymnosporangium species), spread from nearby junipers.Remove nearby juniper hosts if possible; spray preventatively with a copper fungicide.
Fire blight — shoots wilting and turning brownBacterial infection (Erwinia amylovora), especially after warm wet spring.Cut infected shoots 30 cm below visible damage; sterilise tools; apply copper treatment.
Fruit cracking before harvestIrregular watering — dry then sudden heavy irrigation.Maintain consistent soil moisture, especially as fruit nears maturity.
Availability: Widely available in Tunisian nurseries; grown throughout northern and central regions.
