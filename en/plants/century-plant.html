Century plant

Agave americana  ·  Family: Asparagaceae
Exterior · Succulent / cactus
EN Century plant  ·  FR Agave d'Amérique  ·  AR صبّار أمريكي  ·  Agave americana
Origin: Mexico (widely naturalised in Tunisia)
Light
Full sun
Water
Very low
Care
Easy
Safety
Toxic if eaten
Setting
Outdoor
In short Tunisia's most iconic sculptural succulent — its massive blue-grey rosette needs almost no water and endures salt, wind, and drought across every Tunisian region, flowering spectacularly once in its long life.
Light
Full sun — must have maximum sun; deep shade causes etiolation.
Water
Extremely low: once a month or less in summer; no watering in winter for established plants.
Soil
Any well-drained or rocky soil; tolerates sand, chalk, and salt; hates waterlogged clay.
Temperature
Extremely heat-tolerant; handles light frost to about −10 °C once mature.
Humidity
Thrives in dry to semi-arid conditions; excellent coastal plant.
Feeding
None needed for established plants; young plants benefit from a light cactus feed in spring.
Pruning & grooming
Remove dead leaves carefully using thick gloves; remove pups if not wanted.
Propagation
By offsets (pups) at the base; remove when 15–20 cm tall and pot up separately.
Mature size
1.5–2 m tall rosette; flower spike can reach 6–8 m.
Growth rate
Slow — decades to maturity.
Lifespan
Monocarpic: flowers once (usually at 20–40 years), then dies; replaced by offsets.
Bloom / fruit season
Once in lifetime — a towering 6–8 m candelabra spike with yellow flowers; mainly summer.
Hardiness (min temp)
Robust; tolerates frost to about −10 °C in dry conditions once established.
Toxicity & safety
Sap is irritant to skin and eyes; leaf tips are dangerously sharp — plant away from paths.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) Established plants need no fertilizer; for young plants, a single light cactus feed in spring supports initial growth.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Agave snout weevil (sudden collapse)Scyphophorus acupunctatus weevil lays eggs at the base; larvae destroy the core.No cure once advanced; remove and destroy infested plant; do not replant agave in the same spot.
Brown, dying lower leavesNormal — agave naturally drops its lowest leaves.Trim dead leaves close to the stem with thick gloves; no other action needed.
White mealybugs in leaf jointsMealybugs attracted by dry conditions.Spray with dilute soapy water or neem oil; repeat weekly until clear.
Availability: Grows wild throughout Tunisia; young offsets widely available in markets and garden centres.
