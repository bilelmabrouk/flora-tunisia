Sweet cherry

Prunus avium  ·  Family: Rosaceae
Trees · Fruit / nut
EN Sweet cherry  ·  FR Cerisier  ·  AR كرز  ·  Prunus avium
Origin: Europe and western Asia (native from Britain to the Caucasus)
Light
Full sun
Water
Low
Care
Demanding
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short Sweet cherry demands the coolest, wettest parts of Tunisia — the north and north-west highlands — where chilling hours and winter rain combine. Rewarding but demanding, needing cross-pollination from a compatible variety.
Light
Full sun; at least 6 hours daily; but needs afternoon shade in extreme summer heat.
Water
Moderate; regular during establishment; slightly drought-tolerant when mature; avoid water stress at fruit fill.
Soil
Deep, fertile, well-drained; slightly alkaline to neutral; dislikes heavy clay and wet soils.
Temperature
Hardy to −25 °C; needs 800–1400 chilling hours below 7 °C; late frosts damage open flowers.
Humidity
Moderate; avoid very humid conditions during ripening — promotes brown rot.
Feeding
Balanced fruit-tree fertilizer in early spring; avoid excess nitrogen which delays ripening.
Pruning & grooming
Prune in summer (July–August) to minimise silver leaf disease risk; open pyramid or vase shape.
Propagation
Graft onto wild cherry, Colt or Mahaleb rootstock; cross-pollinator variety essential for most cultivars.
Mature size
5–15 m on standard; 3–5 m on semi-dwarfing rootstock.
Growth rate
Moderate to fast; fruiting from year 4–7.
Lifespan
25–60 years.
Bloom / fruit season
Spectacular white flowers in March–April; glossy red or dark cherries in June–July.
Hardiness (min temp)
Very cold-hardy; best in Tunisia's north and north-west highlands (regions 1, 2).
Toxicity & safety
Fruit safe; leaves and stones (kernels) contain cyanogenic glycosides — keep quantities low.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) Apply a balanced fertilizer in early spring; cherries are sensitive to excess nitrogen — too much produces lush growth and poor fruit colour and sweetness.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Silver sheen on leaves, branch die-backSilver leaf disease (Chondrostereum purpureum) entering through wounds.Prune only in summer (July–August); seal all wounds promptly; destroy affected wood.
Cherries splitting and shrivelling at harvestRain or irrigation after dry period just before harvest causes rapid uptake and skin splitting.Harvest as soon as cherries reach maturity; maintain consistent soil moisture throughout fruiting.
Brown rot on fruit and blossoms in wet springMonilinia laxa or M. fructicola — very common on cherry in humid conditions.Apply copper fungicide at pink bud; remove mummified fruit; improve air circulation.
Availability: Available in Tunisian nurseries mainly in the north; low-chill varieties increasingly offered.
