Apricot

Prunus armeniaca  ·  Family: Rosaceae
Trees · Fruit / nut
EN Apricot  ·  FR Abricotier  ·  AR مشماش  ·  Prunus armeniaca
Origin: Central Asia (likely northern China and central Asian highlands)
Light
Full sun
Water
Low
Care
Moderate
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short One of the most beloved fruit trees in Tunisia — the apricot's early spring blossom and sweet summer fruit make it a garden classic. It performs best in the north and highlands where chilling hours can be met.
Light
Full sun for at least 6–8 hours; shaded trees rarely fruit well.
Water
Moderate; regular watering during establishment and fruiting; fairly drought-tolerant once mature.
Soil
Well-drained, moderately fertile; slightly alkaline to neutral suits it well; avoid waterlogged positions.
Temperature
Hardy to −20 °C but spring flowers vulnerable to late frost; needs 400–900 chilling hours below 7 °C.
Humidity
Low to moderate; humid air encourages brown rot on fruit.
Feeding
Balanced fruit-tree fertilizer in early spring; avoid excess nitrogen in summer.
Pruning & grooming
Open-vase shape; annual winter pruning to remove dead wood and maintain open crown.
Propagation
By grafting onto apricot, peach or plum rootstock; hardwood cuttings sometimes used.
Mature size
4–8 m; easily maintained at 3–5 m.
Growth rate
Moderate to fast; begins fruiting from year 3–5.
Lifespan
25–50 years in good conditions.
Bloom / fruit season
Beautiful white-pink flowers in February–March; ripe orange-gold fruit in June–July.
Hardiness (min temp)
Very frost-hardy but late-spring frosts can destroy the blossom — choose a sheltered site.
Toxicity & safety
Fruit and flesh safe; the kernel inside the stone contains amygdalin — not for consumption in quantity.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) Apply a balanced fruit-tree fertilizer in early spring just as buds open; a second lighter application of potassium-rich fertilizer after fruit set helps develop fruit quality.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Brown, rotting fruit remaining on treeBrown rot (Monilinia laxa) — the main disease of apricot in Tunisia's humid spring.Remove all mummified fruit; apply copper fungicide at pink bud stage and again at full bloom.
Gummy discharge from bark (gummosis)Bacterial canker, physical damage or fungal infection at wound sites.Remove cankered wood; seal wounds promptly; avoid tools that damage bark.
Shot-hole on leaves — small brown circles with holesBacterial shot-hole (Xanthomonas arboricola) or fungal Wilsonomyces.Apply copper spray at leaf fall and at bud swell; remove fallen leaves.
Availability: Widely available in Tunisian nurseries; many local and imported varieties suited to different regions.
