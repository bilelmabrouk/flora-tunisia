Black mulberry

Morus nigra  ·  Family: Moraceae
Trees · Fruit / nut
EN Black mulberry  ·  FR Mûrier noir  ·  AR توت أسود  ·  Morus nigra
Origin: South-western Asia (Iran, Afghanistan, Caucasus region)
Light
Full sun
Water
Low
Care
Easy
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short Prized for its intensely sweet-sour dark berries, the black mulberry is a long-lived, low-maintenance tree well-suited to Tunisia's north and centre. Its fruit stains everything — harvest with care.
Light
Full sun for the best fruit quality and sweetness.
Water
Low to moderate; drought-tolerant once established; young trees need more frequent watering.
Soil
Well-drained, moderately fertile; tolerates clay and calcareous soils; avoids waterlogging.
Temperature
Hardy to about −20 °C; needs moderate chilling in winter; excels in continental climate of regions 1–4.
Humidity
Moderate; not as heat-tolerant as white mulberry in extreme southern Tunisia.
Feeding
Light annual feed with balanced fertilizer in early spring; mature trees rarely need more.
Pruning & grooming
Prune in late winter; keep crown open; remove dead, diseased or rubbing branches. Bleeds sap — avoid excessive pruning.
Propagation
Hardwood cuttings in winter (slower to root than M. alba); grafting for selected varieties.
Mature size
4–10 m; often gnarled and picturesque with age.
Growth rate
Slow to moderate; starts fruiting around year 4–6.
Lifespan
Very long-lived — 300–500+ years.
Bloom / fruit season
Catkins in spring; dark purple-black fruit ripen June–August.
Hardiness (min temp)
Very frost-hardy; thrives in regions 1–4; may struggle in the extreme south.
Toxicity & safety
Ripe fruit entirely safe; unripe green fruit can cause mild digestive upset; white sap mildly irritating.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) The black mulberry is self-sufficient in reasonable soil; a light balanced fertilizer in early spring every 1–2 years supports steady fruiting without over-stimulating vegetative growth.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Berries fail to ripen uniformlyUneven watering or insufficient heat in northern highlands.Maintain consistent moisture during fruit development; choose a warm, sheltered position.
White powdery mildew on leavesPhyllactinia fungus in warm dry conditions.Apply sulphur fungicide; improve air circulation by light pruning.
Sap weeping from trunk or branchesPruning wounds, bark beetle entry points, or bacterial canker.Seal pruning wounds; avoid unnecessary cuts; remove severely cankered wood.
Availability: Available in Tunisian nurseries, mainly in northern regions; less common than white mulberry.
