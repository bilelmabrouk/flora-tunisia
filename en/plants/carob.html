Carob

Ceratonia siliqua  ·  Family: Fabaceae
Trees · Fruit / nut
EN Carob  ·  FR Caroubier  ·  AR خرّوب  ·  Ceratonia siliqua
Origin: Mediterranean native (eastern Mediterranean and Middle East)
Light
Full sun
Water
Very low
Care
Easy
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short A tough, drought-hardy evergreen native to the Mediterranean that thrives across most of Tunisia. Carob pods are a traditional Tunisian food and the tree needs almost no watering once established.
Light
Full sun is essential; very intolerant of shade.
Water
Very drought-tolerant once established; young trees need occasional deep watering in summer.
Soil
Well-drained, even poor and stony soil; thrives on calcareous and sandy soils common in Tunisia.
Temperature
Heat-loving; tolerates mild frost down to about −7 °C; best in warm, dry climates.
Humidity
Prefers low humidity and dry air; humid conditions can cause fungal issues.
Feeding
Rarely needed; a light balanced feed every few years is sufficient.
Pruning & grooming
Light shaping when young; mature trees need minimal pruning — remove dead wood only.
Propagation
From seed (scarify hard coat before sowing); grafting used for selected pod-bearing varieties.
Mature size
5–10 m tall with a broad, dense crown up to 10 m wide.
Growth rate
Slow to moderate; takes 6–10 years to begin fruiting.
Lifespan
Exceptionally long-lived — hundreds of years possible.
Bloom / fruit season
Small inconspicuous flowers in autumn (Sept–Nov); pods mature the following autumn.
Hardiness (min temp)
Hardy to about −7 °C; avoid frost-prone highland areas (region 2 risky in cold winters).
Toxicity & safety
Fully safe — pods are edible and widely used; seeds and bark harmless.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) Carob rarely needs fertilizing; if soil is very poor, apply a small amount of balanced fertilizer in early spring every 2–3 years.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Gummy dark lesions on podsBotrytis or other fungal rot in humid conditions.Improve air flow; remove affected pods; treat with sulphur-based fungicide.
Leaf scorch and wilting in summerIn young trees: under-watering or root damage during transplanting.Deep water young trees weekly in summer heat; avoid root disturbance.
No pods on established treeMay be a male tree (carob trees can be male, female, or hermaphrodite).Plant a female or hermaphrodite variety nearby; graft a fruiting variety onto the existing tree.
Availability: Commonly grown across Tunisia; seedlings available in nurseries nationwide.
