Medlar

Mespilus germanica  ·  Family: Rosaceae
Trees · Fruit / nut
EN Medlar  ·  FR Néflier commun  ·  AR زعرور  ·  Mespilus germanica
Origin: South-western Asia and south-eastern Europe
Light
Full sun
Water
Low
Care
Easy
Safety
Pet & child safe
Setting
Outdoor
In short A charming, low-maintenance deciduous tree with white spring blossom and russet autumn fruit, suited to the cooler north of Tunisia. The fruit is eaten after bletting (frost-softening) and is largely forgotten in modern gardens.
Light
Full sun for best fruiting; tolerates light partial shade.
Water
Moderate; fairly drought-tolerant once established; water during prolonged dry spells.
Soil
Adaptable to most well-drained soils; tolerates clay and slightly calcareous conditions.
Temperature
Hardy to −15 °C; needs chilling hours; late frosts can damage spring flowers.
Humidity
Average; tolerates moderate humidity; good air circulation reduces fungal risk.
Feeding
Light balanced fertilizer in early spring; established trees need very little.
Pruning & grooming
Minimal pruning; remove dead wood and any crossing branches in late winter.
Propagation
By seed (slow, 2–3 years) or grafting onto hawthorn or quince rootstock.
Mature size
3–6 m tall with a broad, somewhat irregular crown.
Growth rate
Slow to moderate; fruits from year 4–6.
Lifespan
Long-lived — 50–100+ years.
Bloom / fruit season
Attractive single white flowers in April–May; brown russet fruit in October–November.
Hardiness (min temp)
Very frost-hardy; well-suited to highlands and north Tunisia.
Toxicity & safety
Fully safe; the bleted fruit has a sweet, wine-like flavour.
Fertilizer (Tunisia) Medlar is undemanding; a light annual dose of balanced fruit-tree fertilizer in early spring is sufficient and avoids over-stimulating vegetative growth.  Find fertilizer →

Troubleshooting
SymptomLikely causeWhat to do
Brown, rotting fruit still on treeBrown rot fungus (Monilinia) in warm damp autumn.Remove and destroy mummified fruit; prune to improve air circulation.
Rust spots on leaves — orange pustulesQuince/medlar rust (Gymnosporangium) transmitted from juniper.Apply copper fungicide in spring; avoid planting near juniper trees.
Leaves with fire blight-like die-backBacterial fire blight (Erwinia amylovora) — medlar is susceptible.Prune 30 cm below visible infection; sterilise tools; apply copper spray.
Availability: Uncommon in Tunisian nurseries; occasionally found in specialist nurseries in the north.
